01Feature to Story

SELECT and Fetch went live together this spring. The announcement explains the plumbing, not the pitch.

Media.net brought Fetch’s receipt-level purchase data into two of its own products this year: SELECT, its curation platform, for audience activation, and ELEVATE, its measurement product, for closing the loop between exposure and an actual purchase. The trade press covered it as a data integration. It reads like one. It should read like the thing every open-web buyer has been missing.

Trade coverage · framing as announced

Fetch becomes the first in a planned network of commerce data partners inside SELECT. Its users scan a large volume of receipts daily, and that transaction-level data now feeds SELECT for audience segments and ELEVATE for measurement through a privacy-safe clean room. The coverage is careful, technical, and mostly about the mechanism: retailer-agnostic data, DSP-agnostic activation, a clean room in the middle.

reframe, mine
Every open-web buyer has been told what someone might buy. Now Media.net can show what they actually did.

Media.net has always sold two kinds of signal: the page someone’s reading, through ContextGraph, and what they’re actively searching for, through SearchSignals. Both are strong proxies. Neither is a receipt. Fetch is the first time a Media.net product can point at an actual, scanned purchase and say this is what came of it, on the open web, without a retailer’s walled garden in the way. That's the headline: not "new data partner," but "the open web finally gets the one signal it never had."

Why this works: it puts the buyer’s actual frustration first, that open-web performance has always been argued from inferred intent, and makes the technical integration the proof, not the pitch.
Sample · launch post

“For years, open-web advertisers have optimized against everything except the thing that actually matters: did someone buy. Retail media networks have the receipts, but only for their own four walls. We just brought the receipt to the open web, retailer-agnostic, and wired it straight into the same curation stack that already reads context and search intent. Same reach. New proof.”

Note: the mechanics above (SELECT, ELEVATE, ContextGraph, SearchSignals, the Fetch partnership) are drawn from Media.net’s own site and public trade coverage. The framing, the headline, and the sample post are mine.

03Launch Plan

If the Fetch and World Cup story were mine to ship, here’s the six weeks around it.

The tournament window is a live, time-boxed moment, and Fetch is already integrated. Here’s the plan I’d run to connect the two before the moment passes, and how I’d know it worked.

Week −2

Enable the team

  • Brief the DSP and publisher account teams on the Fetch-enabled curated deal package
  • Ship a one-pager: how to request a Fetch-layered deal ID this week, not next quarter
  • Pre-brief two or three advertiser partners already running World Cup creative for early access
Week −1

Build the assets

  • Lock the launch post and the 30-second explainer from Part 02
  • One-page comparison sheet: what changes for a buyer adding Fetch to an existing SearchSignals deal
  • Deals Desk banner and publisher-side changelog note pointing to the new deal IDs
Tournament window

Ship it during the moment

  • Post and explainer go out on LinkedIn, the press room, and directly through the Deals Desk
  • Sales push timed to the semifinal and final viewership spike, while attention is highest
  • Line up one willing advertiser partner for a short case write-up once the tournament ends
Week +1

Push adoption past the moment

  • Track which DSPs actually pull a Fetch-enabled deal ID first, and which don’t
  • Direct nudge to buyers already running curated deals without the Fetch layer
  • Office hours for account teams fielding the buyer questions this raises
Success metrics How I’d know, by week 4, if this actually moved anything
Awareness

Impressions and shares on the launch post and the explainer

Activation

Fetch-tagged deal ID requests through the Deals Desk

Sales

Number of DSPs pulling a Fetch-enabled segment in week 1

Pipeline

Curated deal value with the Fetch layer vs. without it, tracked separately

Product

Repeat Fetch-layered deal requests from the same buyer at 30 and 60 days

04Position

Why wouldn’t a buyer just go to Magnite or PubMatic instead?

Magnite signed its own commerce data deal, with Expedia’s travel data, about a week before Media.net announced Fetch. That’s not a coincidence, it’s where the whole sell side is heading. Here’s the honest read on the four names most likely to come up in this exact conversation.

SSP
What it’s built around
Why Media.net still holds up
PubMatic
Independent exchange scale and auction infrastructure, built for programmatic efficiency first.
Deep on plumbing, thinner on an owned first-party signal stack. Media.net came from contextual advertising, not exchange tech, so context and search are native to it, not bolted on.
Magnite
CTV-heavy scale, now adding category-specific commerce data deals like the Expedia travel data partnership.
Building data pipes one category deal at a time. Media.net’s SearchSignals and ContextGraph already existed before Fetch, so the new data layers onto a signal stack instead of starting one.
Sovrn
Strong publisher-side tooling and its own contextual and content-licensing products.
Smaller DSP footprint. Media.net has direct integrations across six major demand-side platforms, including The Trade Desk and DV360, which most contextual-first players its size don’t match.
TripleLift
Creative-native ad formats built to blend into publisher content.
Format-first, not signal-first. Media.net’s pitch isn’t a better-looking ad unit, it’s better proof of who saw it and what they did after.
None of these are the wrong tool. They’re building from different starting points: Magnite from CTV supply, TripleLift from creative formats, PubMatic from exchange scale. Media.net’s starting point was contextual signal from its Yahoo and Bing days, and Fetch is the newest layer on a stack that was already built to hold it.
